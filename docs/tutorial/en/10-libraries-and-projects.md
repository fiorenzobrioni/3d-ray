# Chapter 10: Presets and Projects

3D-Ray ships with a rich catalogue of ready-made building blocks: hundreds
of materials, a dozen lighting setups (plus emissive geometry-light
presets), participating-media recipes, terrain recipes, image textures, and
3D font templates. This chapter shows you how to use them, gives you the
full CLI reference, and walks through building a real project.

The materials, lights, mediums, world, sky, and terrain presets are
**copy-paste catalogues** under `scenes/presets/` — Markdown files full of
validated YAML blocks. The binary assets they reference (image textures,
font templates, heightmaps) live under `scenes/assets/`.

---

## 10.1 The Preset Catalogues

All preset catalogues live in `scenes/presets/` as Markdown files. You open
the one you need, copy a YAML block, and paste it into your scene:

```
scenes/presets/
  README.md                  How the copy-paste workflow works
  world.md                   Sky + ground pairings (+ optional medium)
  sky.md                     Sky models in isolation (flat, gradient, Preetham, Nishita, Hosek, HDRI)
  materials-stone.md         Marbles, granites, travertine, onyx, alabaster, brick, concrete
  materials-metal.md         Raw and polished metals, industrial coatings
  materials-wood.md          Raw, lacquered, painted woods
  materials-glass.md         Glass, gems/minerals, liquids (transmissive family)
  materials-organic.md       Fabrics, leathers, foods, organics
  materials-synthetic.md     Plastics, synthetics, paints, ceramics
  materials-ground.md        Ground materials (pair with world.md)
  materials-weathering.md    Weathering overlays and `mix` recipes
  lights.md                  Ready light rigs (3-point, high-key, golden hour, neon, …)
  mediums.md                 Atmospheres, fogs, ice/snow, SSS, volumetric liquids
  terrains.md                Heightfield recipe + altitude/slope strata
```

The binary assets the catalogues reference live under `scenes/assets/`:

```
scenes/assets/
  textures/     PNG image textures (albedo + normal/roughness/AO maps)
  fonts/        3D character templates (generated by FontGen) + .ttf sources
  heightmaps/   16-bit grayscale PNG heightmaps (generated by TerrainGen)
```

### The copy-paste workflow

1. Open the catalogue for the family you need (tables below).
2. Copy the `materials:` / `lights:` / `mediums:` block of the preset you
   want.
3. Paste it into your scene. Entries with the same `id` merge into your
   scene's local section.
4. Reference the `id` from your entities (`material: "..."`) and tweak the
   colour, scale, or roughness to taste.

```yaml
# in your scene
materials:
  - id: "carrara_lucido"        # ← pasted from presets/materials-stone.md
    type: "disney"
    # ...

entities:
  - type: "sphere"
    center: [0, 1, 0]
    radius: 1
    material: "carrara_lucido"
```

Preset `id`s are short and descriptive and are meant to be **renamed**
freely in your scene. Every block is validated against the engine schema
and renders without warnings.

## 10.2 Material Catalogues

Eight themed catalogues cover every surface type you are likely to need.
Most materials use the **Disney BSDF** (`type: disney`); a classic type
(`lambertian`, `metal`, `dielectric`) is used only where it is the correct
choice (e.g. simple background surfaces).

| Catalogue                     | Contents                                       |
|-------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| `materials-stone.md`          | White/dark/coloured marbles, granites, travertine, slate, onyx/alabaster (SSS), basalt, brick, concrete |
| `materials-metal.md`          | Gold, silver, copper, bronze, brass, steel, iron/cast iron, aluminium (incl. anodized), titanium (thin-film), chrome, plus industrial coatings |
| `materials-wood.md`           | Light/medium/dark hardwoods, ebony, exotics, treated (shou-sugi-ban, barnwood), studio figured woods |
| `materials-glass.md`          | Industrial/optical glass, crystal, gems (precious + semiprecious), ice, liquids, frosted |
| `materials-organic.md`        | Fabrics (sheen), leathers, foods, wax, amber, ivory, mother-of-pearl, cork, paper |
| `materials-synthetic.md`      | Plastics, carbon fibre (anisotropic), kevlar, silicones, paints, ceramics, porcelain |
| `materials-ground.md`         | Grass, undergrowth, desert sand, dry earth, rock, gravel, fresh snow, water — pairs with `world.md` |
| `materials-weathering.md`     | `over_*` overlays for `type: mix` (rust, moss, dust, limescale) and ready `mix_*` recipes |

### Conventions

Most materials are `type: disney` (full PBR with clearcoat, sheen,
`spec_trans`, `thin_film`). Pair a Disney material with an `interior_medium`
binding on the entity for Random Walk SSS. The weathering catalogue uses two
families of ids:

- **`over_`** — a weathering overlay, combined via `type: mix` over a base
  material with a procedural mask.
- **`mix_`** — a ready-to-use composite recipe (base + overlay + mask).

### Usage

Copy the blocks you want straight into your scene's `materials:` section,
then reference them by `id`:

```yaml
materials:
  - id: "oro_lucido"            # ← pasted from presets/materials-metal.md
    type: "disney"
    color: [1.0, 0.71, 0.29]
    metallic: 1.0
    roughness: 0.05
    specular: 0.8

  - id: "diamante"             # ← pasted from presets/materials-glass.md
    type: "disney"
    # ...

entities:
  - type: "sphere"
    center: [0, 1, 0]
    radius: 1.0
    material: "oro_lucido"

  - type: "sphere"
    center: [2, 0.5, 0]
    radius: 0.5
    material: "diamante"
```

### Customizing a preset

Because you paste the block into your own scene, customizing is just
editing the values you copied — change `color`, `roughness`, or `scale`
in place, and rename the `id` to whatever fits your scene:

```yaml
materials:
  # A warmer, glossier gold based on the polished-gold preset
  - id: "my_gold"
    type: "disney"
    color: [0.98, 0.8, 0.3]
    metallic: 1.0
    roughness: 0.08
```

## 10.3 Lighting Catalogue (`lights.md`)

`lights.md` is organized into sections by environment. Copy a section's
`lights:` block (and, where suggested, the matching `world:` block) into
your scene:

### Studio and product

| Section            | Setup                | Atmosphere                 |
|--------------------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| 3-point            | Classic 3-point      | Universal product/portrait |
| High-key           | High key             | Clean, commercial, fashion |
| Dramatic (low-key) | Low key/Chiaroscuro  | Noir, dramatic shadows     |
| Product            | Product/Jewelry      | Precise catchlights        |

### Outdoor and night

| Section      | Setup        | Atmosphere                |
|--------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Golden hour  | Warm sun     | Warm cinematic glow       |
| Noon         | Noon sun     | Hard light, short shadows |
| Overcast     | Overcast     | Soft, even, no hard shadows|
| Moonlight    | Moonlit night| Blue-cold, mysterious     |
| Neon/Cyberpunk| Neon         | Sci-fi, vibrant colours   |

### Usage

```yaml
lights:                          # ← pasted from presets/lights.md (3-point)
  - type: "area"
    # key light ...
  - type: "area"
    # fill light ...
  - type: "area"
    # rim light ...

# Each light section suggests a matching world config in its notes.
world:
  sky:
    type: "flat"
    color: [0.0, 0.0, 0.0]
```

### Geometry lights (emissive)

The "Geometry lights (emissive)" section of `lights.md` contains `emi_*`
emissive **materials** that turn any geometry into a NEE-participating light
source — without adding an explicit light entity. They cover the blackbody
range (candle 2000 K → cool-white 7000 K) plus special sources (fire,
embers, LED strips, bioluminescence, direct sun disk).

Copy the `materials:` block and apply the material to any geometry. The
engine automatically registers the geometry as a `GeometryLight` and
includes it in NEE sampling:

```yaml
materials:
  - id: "emi_tungsteno"         # ← pasted from presets/lights.md
    type: "emissive"
    # ...

entities:
  # A warm tungsten-temperature sphere lamp
  - type: "sphere"
    center: [0, 3, 0]
    radius: 0.15
    material: "emi_tungsteno"
```

Tip: geometry lights participate in power-weighted NEE automatically.
For scenes with many geometry lights, `--light-sampling power` reduces
variance when light intensities differ widely.

### Light Hardening: cut fireflies without raising spp

Every light preset is calibrated with the engine's *light hardening* knobs
(see DEVLOG §Light Hardening Cycle and
`docs/reference/scene-reference.md` §8). Three key parameters:

- **`soft_radius`** (point, spot, area) — models the source's physical
  diameter. The `1/d²` term (and `cosLight/d²` on area lights) is floored
  at `max(d², r²)`, removing the persistent fireflies that appear when a
  ray (or a scattering event in fog) lands very close to the emitter.
  Typical values: 0.05–0.20 for bulbs, 0.10–0.30 for neon or reflectors,
  0.20 for a softbox in fog.
- **`angular_radius`** (directional) — angular diameter in degrees of the
  sun/moon disc. `0.27` = real Sun, `0.5` = full Moon. When set, it
  produces physical penumbras (cone sampling, internal `shadow_samples`
  4) instead of infinitely sharp shadows. The outdoor presets already use
  it on every sun/moon.
- **`shadow_samples`** (spot, area, sphere) — jittered visibility samples.
  On `spot`, this only matters when `soft_radius > 0`.

For heavy volumetric scenes (procedural/grid media, dense `height_fog`)
also consider these CLI flags:

```
--indirect-clamp-factor 0.25   # clamp indirect bounces to 1/4 of -C
--light-sampling power         # power-weighted single-light NEE
                               # (variance reduction with mixed-brightness
                               # lights, e.g. 1 area + 10 dim points)
```

In short: before raising `-s` to chase noise, check whether fireflies
come from an unclamped `1/d²` (they show as isolated very bright pixels
near the source). If so, `soft_radius` is the right cure — free and
physically consistent.

## 10.4 Image Textures (`scenes/assets/textures/`)

The `scenes/assets/textures/` folder holds the bundled PNG image textures,
referenced by relative path from your scene file.

### Albedo + map sets
- `brick-wall.png` + `brick-wall-normal.png` (+ roughness, AO)
- `brick-wall-white.png` (shares `brick-wall-normal.png`)
- `concrete.png` + `concrete-normal.png` (+ roughness, AO)
- `metal-scratched.png` + `metal-scratched-normal.png` (+ roughness, metallic)
- `wood-floor.png` + `wood-floor-normal.png` (+ roughness, AO)
- `wood-planks.png` + `wood-planks-normal.png` (+ roughness, AO)
- `earth.png` + `earth-roughness.png`

### Normal maps only
- `fabric-weave-normal.png` — woven texture overlay
- `stone-cobble-normal.png` — cobblestone paving
- `tiles-normal.png` — tile grout lines
- `flat-normal.png` — neutral flat (disables normal mapping cleanly)

### Specialty
- `checkerboard.png` — UV testing
- `grid-uv.png` — numbered UV verification grid
- `logo-3dray.png` — engine logo

### Usage

```yaml
materials:
  - id: "brick_wall"
    type: "disney"
    roughness: 0.7
    texture:
      type: "image"
      path: "assets/textures/brick-wall.png"
      uv_scale: [2, 2]
    normal_map:
      path: "assets/textures/brick-wall-normal.png"
      strength: 1.0
      uv_scale: [2, 2]
```

## 10.5 3D Font Templates (`scenes/assets/fonts/`)

The `scenes/assets/fonts/` directory contains 3D character template files
generated by the `FontGen` tool. Each file covers one font family and
includes templates for uppercase letters (A–Z), lowercase letters (a–z),
and digits (0–9). The matching `.ttf` source fonts live under
`scenes/assets/fonts/ttf/`.

Example file: `assets/fonts/font-open-sans.yaml` — Open Sans family.

A font template file is one of the few things you genuinely *import*
(rather than copy-paste), because it carries dozens of extrusion templates.
Reference a character template using `type: "instance"`:

```yaml
imports:
  - path: "assets/fonts/font-open-sans.yaml"

materials:
  # Define or override the font material used by all characters
  - id: "font_material"
    type: "disney"
    color: [0.9, 0.85, 0.7]
    metallic: 0.0
    roughness: 0.3

entities:
  - type: "instance"
    template: "lettera_A_maiusc_open-sans"
    translate: [0, 0, 0]
    scale: [1, 1, 1]

  - type: "instance"
    template: "lettera_b_minusc_open-sans"
    translate: [0.7, 0, 0]
```

Each template uses a material named `font_material`. Redefine it in your
scene to apply your own surface to all characters without touching the
font file.

### Generating font templates

Use the `FontGen` tool to create new font files from any system font or
.ttf/.otf file:

```
dotnet run --project src/Tools/FontGen/FontGen.csproj -- \
  --font "Open Sans" --height 0.2 --chars "HELLO"
```

Use `--list-fonts` to see all available system fonts. Generated templates
are written to `scenes/assets/fonts/`.

## 10.6 Terrain Heightfields (`terrains.md` + `scenes/assets/heightmaps/`)

The `terrains.md` catalogue provides a copy-paste heightfield recipe: a
grayscale heightmap (16-bit PNG under `scenes/assets/heightmaps/`) is
intersected directly by the engine as a `heightfield` primitive — no mesh
tessellation — with materials assigned to altitude/slope bands (`strata`).

The engine intersects the heightfield directly via a min/max mipmap
quadtree, so one primitive replaces an entire terrain mesh.

### As a ground dispatcher (`world.ground`)

Paste the ground materials and the `world.ground` block from the catalogue;
the heightfield becomes the scene's floor:

```yaml
world:
  ground:
    type: "heightfield"
    heightmap_path: "assets/heightmaps/heightfield-strata-test-height.png"
    bounds: [-50, -50, 50, 50]
    height_scale: 25.0
    strata:
      - { min_altitude: 0.0, max_altitude: 0.36, min_slope_deg: 0, max_slope_deg: 35, blend_width: 0.04, material: "t_sand" }
      - { min_altitude: 0.5, max_altitude: 1.0,  min_slope_deg: 0, max_slope_deg: 90, blend_width: 0.08, material: "t_rock" }
    material: "t_ground"
```

### As a placeable entity

The same fields work on a `type: heightfield` entity, which you can position
and transform anywhere in the scene:

```yaml
entities:
  - type: "heightfield"
    heightmap_path: "assets/heightmaps/heightfield-strata-test-height.png"
    bounds: [-50, -50, 50, 50]
    height_scale: 25.0
    material: "t_ground"
```

### Generating heightmaps

Use the `TerrainGen` tool to create new heightmaps (and a ready-to-paste
terrain template):

```
dotnet run --project src/Tools/TerrainGen/TerrainGen.csproj -- \
  --name my-hills --type collina --season estate --with-cameras
```

`--type` accepts `pianura`, `collina`, or `montagna`. The tool writes a
`<name>-height.png` heightmap to `scenes/assets/heightmaps/`; with
`--with-cameras` it also emits a ready-to-render `scenes/<name>-preview.yaml`.

## 10.7 CLI Reference

The complete set of command-line parameters:

| Flag | Long form          | Default                       | Description                                              |
|------|--------------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| `-i` | `--input`          | *(required)*                  | Path to the scene YAML file                              |
| `-o` | `--output`         | `renders/render-<scene>.png`  | Output image path (PNG, JPG, or BMP)                     |
| `-w` | `--width`          | `960` (draft-small)           | Image width in pixels                                    |
| `-H` | `--height`         | `540` (draft-small)           | Image height in pixels                                   |
| `-s` | `--samples`        | `16` (draft-small)            | Samples per pixel (Sobol: exact count; PRNG: rounded up to nearest perfect square) |
| `-d` | `--depth`          | `4` (draft-small; `8` in quality presets — raise to 16+ for stacked glass) | Maximum ray bounces |
| `-S` | `--shadow-samples` | *(per light)*                 | Override shadow samples for all area/sphere lights (perfect squares) |
| `-C` | `--clamp`          | `10`                          | Firefly clamp: max per-sample radiance before tone mapping |
| `-c` | `--camera`         | `0`                           | Select camera by name or zero-based index                |
|      | `--sampler`        | `sobol`                       | Per-pixel sampler: `sobol` (Owen-scrambled) or `prng`    |
|      | `--mis`            | `balance`                     | MIS heuristic: `balance` or `power`                      |
|      | `--light-sampling` | `all`                         | NEE strategy: `all`, `power`, `uniform`                  |
|      | `--indirect-clamp-factor` | `0.25`                 | Indirect-contribution clamp factor, applied once camera-relative (`0.25` → indirect clamp = ¼ of `-C`; `1.0` = off) |
|      | `--texture-filtering` | `auto`                     | Analytic anti-aliasing of procedural / image textures via ray differentials: `auto` / `on` (filtering on) or `off` (point-sampled, for benchmarks) |
|      | `--list-cameras`   |                               | List available cameras and exit                          |
| `-v` | `--verbose`        |                               | Detailed scene-load and Russian-Roulette tuning output    |
| `-h` | `--help`           |                               | Show help                                                |

### Output Format

The format is determined by the file extension:
- `.png` -- PNG (default, lossless)
- `.jpg` / `.jpeg` -- JPEG (lossy, smaller files)
- `.bmp` -- BMP (uncompressed)

### Samples Rounding

Sample rounding depends on the active sampler (`--sampler`, default `sobol`):

- **Sobol (default):** the exact requested count is used — `-s 15` fires exactly 15 samples per pixel.
- **PRNG (`--sampler prng`):** the count is rounded up to the nearest perfect square. The table below shows how PRNG rounds:

| Requested | Actual (PRNG) | Grid     |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| 1         | 1             | 1x1      |
| 10        | 16            | 4x4      |
| 20        | 25            | 5x5      |
| 50        | 64            | 8x8      |
| 100       | 100           | 10x10    |
| 200       | 225           | 15x15    |
| 256       | 256           | 16x16    |

## 10.8 Building a Complete Project: Step by Step

Here is the workflow for creating a scene from scratch using the preset
catalogues:

### Step 1: Set Up World and Camera

```yaml
world:
  sky:
    type: "flat"
    color: [0.0, 0.0, 0.0]

cameras:
  - name: "main"
    position: [0, 2, -6]
    look_at: [0, 1, 0]
    fov: 45
```

### Step 2: Paste in Materials and Lights

Open `presets/materials-metal.md`, `presets/materials-stone.md`, and the
3-point section of `presets/lights.md`, and copy the blocks you want into
your scene's `materials:` and `lights:` sections:

```yaml
materials:
  - id: "carrara_lucido"        # ← presets/materials-stone.md
    type: "disney"
    # ...
  - id: "oro_lucido"            # ← presets/materials-metal.md
    type: "disney"
    # ...

lights:
  # ← 3-point block pasted from presets/lights.md
  - type: "area"
    # ...
```

### Step 3: Add Entities

```yaml
entities:
  # Floor
  - type: "infinite_plane"
    point: [0, 0, 0]
    normal: [0, 1, 0]
    material: "carrara_lucido"

  # A sphere with a pasted material at the center
  - type: "sphere"
    center: [0, 1.0, 0]
    radius: 0.5
    material: "oro_lucido"

  # Custom object
  - type: "sphere"
    center: [0, 0.78, 0]
    radius: 0.15
    material: "diamante"
```

### Step 4: Iterate

Use the three canonical rendering profiles:

```
# Preview — composition / cameras / materials (seconds)
RayTracer -i my-scene.yaml -w 400 -H 225 -s 64 -d 4 -S 1

# Standard — CI/CD and review renders (minutes)
RayTracer -i my-scene.yaml -w 800 -H 450 -s 256 -d 6

# Final — portfolio / README cover quality
RayTracer -i my-scene.yaml -w 1920 -H 1080 -s 1024 -d 8 -S 4
```

For the full explanation of each parameter, Russian Roulette behavior, the
glass-stacked exception that forces `-d 16+`, and the `-C`/`--clamp` firefly
knob, see **[Rendering Profiles Reference](../../reference/rendering-profiles.md)**.

## 10.9 Organizing a Larger Project

Once a scene grows past a couple hundred entities, a little structure pays off:

- **Build your palette from the catalogues.** Paste only the material,
  light, and medium blocks you actually use, and give them scene-specific
  `id`s (`mat_floor`, `mat_wall`, `mat_glass`). This keeps the `materials:`
  section readable.
- **Split your own scene with `imports:`.** Move repeated object templates
  into a separate file and pull it in with `imports:`. Paths are relative
  to the importing file; local definitions override imported ones; `world`
  and `cameras` are never imported. (See Chapter 5 for the full mechanics.)
- **Keep binary assets under `scenes/assets/`.** Image textures, font
  templates, and heightmaps belong in `assets/{textures,fonts,heightmaps}/`
  and are referenced by relative path. Generated assets (FontGen, TerrainGen)
  land there too.
- **Name everything.** Give every entity and template a descriptive `name:`
  so `--verbose` output and warnings are easy to read.

## 10.10 Troubleshooting Guide

### Black Image
- **No lights.** Add lights to the `lights:` section or use emissive
  objects / HDRI sky.
- **All lights have zero intensity.** Check that `intensity` is positive.
- **Camera inside an object.** Move the camera `position` outside all
  geometry.
- **Camera facing the wrong way.** Check `look_at`.
- **`sky.color: [0,0,0]` with no lights.** A scene needs at least one
  light source or a non-zero sky. Setting `sky.type: flat` with
  `color: [0.02, 0.02, 0.02]` gives you a faint global emitter to
  locate geometry even with no explicit lights.

### Scene Looks Flat or Washed Out
- **Sky is too bright relative to your key light.** A flat sky with a
  high `color` (e.g. `[0.5, 0.5, 0.5]`) bounces a lot of fill onto every
  surface; if it dominates your key light you lose shadow contrast.
  Lower the sky colour or raise the key light's `intensity`.
- **No dominant directional light.** A scene lit only by a sky has no
  clear shadow direction; add a key light (directional, area, or sun
  disk inside a gradient sky) with higher intensity to establish
  contrast.
- **All lights are the same color.** Real environments mix warm and cool
  light. Try a warm key light (`[1.0, 0.9, 0.75]`) paired with a cool
  flat sky (`[0.05, 0.07, 0.1]`).

### Too Much Noise
- Increase samples: `-s 64` or `-s 256`.
- Increase shadow samples: `-S 16`.
- Dense Disney materials (sheen, thin-film) and Random Walk SSS need
  more samples than classic types.
- **Depth of Field is enabled.** A non-zero `aperture` requires many
  more samples to clear bokeh noise. Use at least `-s 256` for clean
  DOF renders.
- **Emissive material inside a CSG node.** The engine warns about this;
  the emissive surface cannot participate in Next Event Estimation and
  causes high variance. Move the emissive primitive outside the CSG tree.

### Very Slow Render
- Reduce resolution and samples during testing.
- Use the preview/draft/final workflow.
- Replace Disney materials with classic equivalents for background
  surfaces.
- **Too many shadow samples.** `-S` cost is multiplicative: `-S 9` with
  two area lights and `-s 256` at 6 bounces is over 27,000 shadow rays
  per pixel. Use `-S 1` or `-S 4` unless you specifically need sharper
  soft shadows.

### Missing Material (Object Appears Default Grey)
- Check for typos in the material `id`.
- Ensure the material is actually defined in your scene's `materials:`
  section (a pasted block must be present, not just referenced).
- Check console warnings for unresolved material references.

### Wrong Colors
- Colors are `[R, G, B]` in the range **0.0--1.0**, not 0--255.
  `[255, 0, 0]` is not red -- it is an extremely bright white.

### Object Is in the Wrong Place or Invisible
- Check the coordinate system: **Y is up**, the floor is at Y = 0.
  Objects placed at negative Y are below the ground.
- A `translate` of `[0, 0, 5]` moves the object **into the scene**
  (positive Z), not toward the camera. To move toward the default
  camera, use negative Z.
- Use `--verbose` to print the scene bounding box and locate lost objects.

### Glass Looks Wrong (Too Dark or Solid)
- Increase ray depth: `-d 16` or higher (glass consumes 2 bounces per
  surface, so stacked or nested glass easily exhausts the default `-d 8`).
- Ensure there is light behind/around the glass object (glass transmits
  light, so it needs something to transmit).

### Textures Not Showing (Magenta/Pink Fallback)
- Check that the texture file path is correct and relative to the scene
  file (e.g. `assets/textures/brick-wall.png`).
- Verify the file exists and is a supported format (PNG, JPEG, BMP).

### Imports Not Working
- Paths are relative to the **importing file**, not the working
  directory.
- Check for circular imports (the engine warns on the console).
- Ensure the imported file has the correct YAML structure (`materials:`,
  `templates:`, etc.).

## 10.11 Complete Example: Exhibition Hall

A scene that combines materials and lights pasted from several catalogues
into a cohesive project.

```yaml
# exhibition-hall.yaml
# A museum-like room showcasing objects from different material families.
# Materials pasted from presets/materials-metal.md and materials-glass.md;
# the emi_daylight material from the geometry-lights section of presets/lights.md.

world:
  sky:
    type: "flat"
    color: [0.0, 0.0, 0.0]

cameras:
```

## What You Have Learned

- The preset catalogues under `scenes/presets/` are copy-paste collections of
  validated YAML: materials, lights, mediums, world/sky pairings, and
  terrain recipes.
- The workflow is open a catalogue → copy a `materials:` / `lights:` /
  `mediums:` block → paste it into your scene → reference the `id` from your
  entities and tweak.
- Emissive `emi_*` materials from the geometry-lights section of `lights.md`
  turn any geometry into a NEE-participating light source — no explicit light
  entity needed.
- Binary assets live under `scenes/assets/`: image textures
  (`assets/textures/`), font templates (`assets/fonts/`, generated by
  FontGen), and heightmaps (`assets/heightmaps/`, generated by TerrainGen).
- `imports:` is for splitting your own scene across files and for pulling in
  generated font templates — not for materials, which you paste in.
- The CLI gives full control over resolution, quality, camera selection, and
  output format.
- The preview/draft/final workflow is the most efficient way to develop
  scenes.
